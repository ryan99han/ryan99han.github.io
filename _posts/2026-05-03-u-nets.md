U-Net is a convolutional neural network architecture designed for image segmentation tasks, originally introduced in [Ronneberger et al., 2015](https://arxiv.org/abs/1505.04597). Despite its simplicity relative to modern architectures, it remains one of the most elegant and effective designs in computer vision.

## Architecture

The key innovation is its **symmetric encoder-decoder structure** with skip connections:

- **Encoder**: Successive convolutions and max-pooling that progressively downsample and extract features
- **Decoder**: Upsampling via transpose convolutions that progressively reconstruct spatial information
- **Skip connections**: Features from encoder layers concatenated with decoder layers at matching resolutions

This symmetry allows the network to:
1. Capture context at multiple scales
2. Recover fine-grained spatial details through skip connections
3. Train effectively even with limited data (which was crucial for biomedical imaging)

## Why it works

The skip connections are the critical insight. Without them, the decoder would need to learn to invert the entire encoding process from a bottleneck representation. With them, the decoder can focus on refining features, reducing the information bottleneck. This is why U-Net generalizes so well across domains beyond its original biomedical focus.

## Modern variants

Many modern segmentation architectures build on U-Net's core ideas:
- **ResUNet**: Adding residual connections
- **DenseUNet**: Replacing with dense blocks
- **3D U-Net**: Extending to volumetric data
- **Attention U-Net**: Adding channel and spatial attention

The architecture's flexibility and strong inductive bias toward segmentation tasks have made it the de-facto standard baseline in the field.
